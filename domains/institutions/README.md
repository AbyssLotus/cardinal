# domains/institutions/

**Charter:** Vol. III Ch. 9 — Institutions. A promise that outlives the people who made
it. Claimed authority, effective capacity, and legitimacy are three separate facts, and
the gaps between them are where political history happens (§9.1).

**Owns:** institutions and offices (identity independent of holders — Ruling 7); laws
(recorded, scoped, sanctioned — and *never* physically preventing anything: law is not
physics, §9.4 Designer Note); jurisdiction; legitimacy (per community); enforcement
capacity; adjudication; treaties and charters; succession rules.

**Never owns:** the people (officials are ordinary persons with private interests),
informal norms (Culture — Ruling 3), the violence itself (Conflict), the treasury's
coins (Economy holdings with an institutional owner), reverence (Culture), territory
as terrain (Physical) (§9.3).

**Key contracts:** offices survive their holders — vacancy is a first-class state;
enforcement requires real agents, real information, real reach; legitimacy changes only
through events; no institution is architecturally sovereign (§9.8).

**Appendix A:** Ruling 3 (norm vs. law), Ruling 7 (office vs. holder), Ruling 10
(money issuance).
